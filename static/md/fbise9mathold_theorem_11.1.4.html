Theorem 11.1.4 ‚Äî Medians of a Triangle Are Concurrent

The medians of a triangle are line segments that connect each vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side. Every triangle has exactly three medians.

This theorem states that all three medians of a triangle meet at a single point. This point is called the centroid of the triangle. Moreover, this point has a special property: it divides each median into two segments such that the segment from the vertex to the centroid is twice as long as the segment from the centroid to the midpoint of the opposite side.

In other words, the medians are not just concurrent ‚Äî their point of concurrency is also the point of trisection for each median.

üîπ What does concurrent mean?
Concurrent lines are lines that all intersect at a single point. The length of the lines does not matter. They do not need to be equal or symmetrical. For example, the three medians of a triangle are concurrent because they all pass through one shared point ‚Äî the centroid.

‚ùå Concurrent does not mean congruent.
Congruent lines are equal in length. Concurrent lines may or may not be congruent ‚Äî the key is that they meet at one point, not that they are the same.
