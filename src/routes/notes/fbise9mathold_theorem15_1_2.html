Theorem 15.1.2 – Converse of Pythagoras Theorem

Book Statement

If, in a triangle, the square on one side equals the sum of the squares on the other two sides, then the angle opposite the first side is a right angle.

Key Vocabulary

  • Converse: A reversed implication; here, from square-sum to right angle.
  • Right angle: An angle of measure 90°.
  • Longest side: In a triangle whose sides satisfy c² = a² + b², the side c is opposite the right angle.

Symbolically, if in triangle ABC we have AB² = AC² + BC², then ∠C = 90°.

What We Will Prove

  1. Given a triangle with side lengths satisfying AB² = AC² + BC², the angle at C is a right angle.
  2. We justify this by constructing a reference right triangle and using congruence.

Detailed Proof

Step 1 – Build a Reference Right Triangle

Let triangle ABC satisfy AB² = AC² + BC². Construct triangle A′B′C′ with a right angle at C′, legs A′C′ = AC and B′C′ = BC. By Pythagoras, its hypotenuse satisfies A′B′² = A′C′² + B′C′² = AC² + BC² = AB², hence A′B′ = AB.

Step 2 – Compare the Two Triangles

Now triangles ABC and A′B′C′ have three corresponding sides equal: AB = A′B′, AC = A′C′, BC = B′C′. Therefore, △ABC ≅ △A′B′C′ by SSS.

Step 3 – Conclude the Angle is Right

Corresponding parts of congruent triangles are equal, so ∠C corresponds to the right angle ∠C′. Hence ∠C = 90°, completing the proof.
