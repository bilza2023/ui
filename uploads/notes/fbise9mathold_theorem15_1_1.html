Theorem 15.1.1 – Pythagoras Theorem

Book Statement

In a right-angled triangle, the square on the hypotenuse is equal to the sum of the squares on the other two sides.

Key Vocabulary

  • Right-angled triangle: A triangle with one angle equal to 90°.
  • Hypotenuse: The side opposite the right angle; the longest side of a right-angled triangle.
  • Legs (or perpendicular and base): The two sides that form the right angle.

Symbolically, if the right angle is at C in triangle ABC, then AB is the hypotenuse and AB² = AC² + BC².

What We Will Prove

  1. In right-angled triangle ABC with right angle at C, the relation AB² = AC² + BC² holds.
  2. This follows from similarity of the two smaller triangles formed by the altitude from the right angle to the hypotenuse.

Detailed Proof

Step 1 – Construct the Altitude

Let ABC be a right-angled triangle with right angle at C. Drop an altitude from C to the hypotenuse AB, meeting it at D.

Step 2 – Establish Similarity

Observe that ∠ACB is a right angle and ∠ADC, ∠CBD are also right angles. Angle chasing gives ∠CAB = ∠CAD and ∠CBA = ∠CBD. Hence △ABC ∼ △ACD and △ABC ∼ △CBD by AA similarity.

Step 3 – Convert Similarity to the Square Relation

From △ABC ∼ △ACD, corresponding sides give AC/AB = AD/AC, so AC² = AD·AB. From △ABC ∼ △CBD, we get BC/AB = BD/BC, so BC² = BD·AB.

Adding these equalities: AC² + BC² = (AD·AB) + (BD·AB) = (AD + BD)·AB = AB·AB = AB², which proves the theorem.

AB^2 = AC^2 + BC^2
