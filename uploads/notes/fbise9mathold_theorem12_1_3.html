Theorem 12.1.3 – The Perpendicular Bisectors of the Sides of a Triangle Are Concurrent

Book Statement

The perpendicular bisectors of the three sides of any triangle meet at a single point (the circumcenter).

Key Vocabulary

  • Perpendicular Bisector: Line through the midpoint of a segment, perpendicular to it.
  • Circumcenter (O): Intersection of a triangle’s perpendicular bisectors; center of the circumscribed circle.

If a point is equidistant from two vertices twice over, it must also be equidistant from the third—forcing all three bisectors through that same point.

Construction

  1. In △ABC, draw the perpendicular bisector of side AB. Call it lAB.
  2. Draw the perpendicular bisector of side BC. Call it lBC.
  3. Let their intersection be O.

Detailed Proof

Step 1 — O Is Equidistant from A and B

By Theorem 12 .1 .1, any point on the perpendicular bisector of a segment is equidistant from the segment’s endpoints. Since O lies on lAB, we have OA = OB.

Step 2 — O Is Equidistant from B and C

Similarly, O lies on lBC; therefore OB = OC.

Step 3 — O Is Equidistant from A and C

From Steps 1 and 2: OA = OB and OB = OC ⇒ OA = OC.

Step 4 — O Lies on the Perpendicular Bisector of AC

Converse of Theorem 12 .1 .2 states: if a point is equidistant from the endpoints of a segment, it lies on that segment’s perpendicular bisector. Since OA = OC, point O must lie on the perpendicular bisector of AC.

Step 5 — Concurrency Established

All three perpendicular bisectors (lAB, lBC, and the bisector of AC) pass through the same point O. Hence they are concurrent.
