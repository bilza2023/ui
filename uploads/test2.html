Kinematics (Motion Without Forces)

Kinematics is the branch of mechanics that describes the motion of objects without considering the causes (forces). It answers: How do things move? but not Why?

1) Core Quantities in Kinematics

  • Distance (s): Total path length travelled (scalar).
  • Displacement (Δx): Change in position in a straight line, with direction (vector).
  • Speed (v): Rate of change of distance: v = s/t (scalar).
  • Velocity (→v): Rate of change of displacement: v = Δx/Δt (vector).
  • Acceleration (a): Rate of change of velocity: a = Δv/Δt.

2) Types of Motion

  • Uniform motion: Constant speed in a straight line.
  • Uniformly accelerated motion: Acceleration is constant (e.g., free fall).
  • Non-uniform motion: Speed or direction keeps changing irregularly.

3) Kinematic Equations (constant a)

  1. v = u + a t
  2. s = u t + ½ a t²
  3. v² = u² + 2 a s

Where u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, a = acceleration, t = time, s = displacement.

4) Graphical Tools

  • Displacement–time graph → slope = velocity
  • Velocity–time graph → slope = acceleration, area = displacement
  • Acceleration–time graph → area = change in velocity
Core idea: Kinematics = motion description. Use equations and graphs to predict where an object will be at any moment.
