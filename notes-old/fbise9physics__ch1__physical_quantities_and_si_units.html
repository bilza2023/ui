Physical Quantities & SI Units

Physical quantity = any property that can be measured and expressed as a number with a unit (e.g., 5 m, 12 s, 3.2 kg).

1) Types of Physical Quantities

  • Base (fundamental) quantities: Defined independently; form the foundation of the SI system.
  • Derived quantities: Built from base quantities via formulas (e.g., speed = distance/time).

2) SI Units — Usage Rules (Quick)

  • Always keep a space between number and unit: 25 m, 3.0 s.
  • Unit symbols don’t take plural: 5 kg (not 5 kgs).
  • No full stop after a unit symbol: m, s, N.
  • Unit names are lowercase (except proper names): newton, joule; symbols may be uppercase: N, J.
  • Use prefixes with units (not with quantities): km, ms, μm.
  • Be careful with squared/cubed units in conversions (e.g., cm² → m²).

4) Mini Examples (clean & exam-style)

  1. Speed to SI: 72 km h⁻1
    → 72 × (1000 m)/(3600 s) = 20 m s⁻1
  2. Area conversion: 450 cm²
    → 450 × (10⁻2 m)² = 450 × 10⁻4 m² = 0.045 m²
  3. Volume conversion: 2500 cm³
    → 2500 × (10⁻2 m)³ = 2500 × 10⁻6 m³ = 2.5 × 10⁻3 m³
  4. Mass: 750 mg
    → 750 × 10⁻6 kg = 7.5 × 10⁻4 kg
  5. Time: 250 μs
    → 250 × 10⁻6 s = 2.5 × 10⁻4 s

5) Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Writing 5kgs or 20m. (wrong) — use 5 kg, 20 m.
  • For squared/cubed units, remember the power applies to the whole prefix+unit (e.g., 1 cm² = 10⁻4 m²).
  • Mixing systems (e.g., km with min) without converting both to SI.
Core idea: Every measurement = number × SI unit. Keep units consistent to avoid errors.
